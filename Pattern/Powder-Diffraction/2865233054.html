Principles of Powder Diffraction

Posted on 06-25-2020 In Pattern , Powder Diffraction Waline: Word count in article: 559 Reading time ≈ 2 mins.

Powder diffraction is an awesome technique using X-ray, neutron, or electron diffraction on powder or microcrystalline samples for structural characterization of materials. Single crystal analysis is the preferred method for determining crystal structures, but when suitable single crystals are unavailable, powder diffraction is an effective alternative. Unlike single crystal analysis, powder diffraction also provides information on bulk material and microstructural properties. Nowadays, Powder diffraction continues to be a crucial tool in the characterization of materials.

In fact, measuring X-ray diffraction involves detecting the intensity of X-rays scattered by bound electrons. The electromagnetic waves scattered by atoms at different positions arrive at the detector with a relative phase shift. Therefore, the measured intensity provides information about the relative positions of the atoms.

Bragg Equation

X-rays can be reflected by sets of lattice planes in crystals through a process known as X-ray diffraction. This phenomenon occurs because the wavelengths of X-rays are similar in size to the spacing between the lattice planes in a crystal.

Derivation

Bragg analysis posits that X-rays are specularly reflected by lattice planes. Unlike visible light reflected by a mirror, X-rays can penetrate deep inside the material where additional reflections occur across thousands of constructive parallel planes. Since all X-rays are reflected in the same direction, a superposition of the scattered rays occurs.

The constructive interference will occur only if is a integer multiple of the wavelength of X-rays. There is always a plane such that the equals an odd multiple of half the wavelength, which leads to destructive interference. Thus, sharp intensity maxima emerge from the specimen only at the special angles when constructive interference occurs, with no intensity in between.

In general, atoms in one plane will not lie exactly above atoms in the plane below.
